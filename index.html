Julia C. Geue

Conservation and ecological/landscape geneticist with a love for Bumble bees and a strong interest in bioinformatics.

I aim to apply my knowledge in conservation genomics and spatial biodiversity analysis to the conservation of biodiversity in times of global change. Combining genomics and conservation modeling in our constantly changing world is in my opinion extremely important in order to take informed conservation decisions. I am particularly interested in the question how adaptive genetic variation associated to the environment can be informative and used for conservation prioritization. I aim to contribute to the body of evidence that the genetic level of biodiversity, which was rather “neglected” in the past, is an important factor in nature conservation.

I'm currently a postdoctoral Fellow at Umeå university and my research focusses on two closely related and hybrdizing conifer species; Norway and Siberian Spruce under the mentorship of Prof. Xiao-Ru Wang.

Research and Interests

My main interest lies in conserving our beautiful mother nature and everything connected to it. When we talk about biodiversity we should include all levels, such as genes, species and environment. My research projects range from conservation genetics, adaptation, comparative and landscape genomics to applied conservation work.

Genomics for biodiversity conservation

As an active member in the IUCN ‘Conservation Genetics Specialist Group’ (CGSG), I am engaged in the promotion of the use of genetics and genomics in conservation management and decision making. We are trying to establish guidelines how to best implement genetics in practical conservation as well as enlarging and connecting the conservation genetics community all around the world.

Bumble bees – from species distributions to local adaptation

I worked on two bumble bee species in Eastern Europe (Bombus terrestris and Bombus lucorum). These closely related species are very similar in their morphology and life-history, but their ranges only partly overlap, raising the question how they are ecologically divergent. To disentangle their broad habitat preferences, I used species distribution modeling and machine learning approaches. I also conducted low coverage whole genome sequencing and showed a strong influence of the environment on regions of the genome in both species, and evaluated how potential local adaptation could contribute to differences in their distributions.


Spatial conservation prioritization

Biodiveristy is very complex and it is therefore difficult to comprehensively characterize biodiversity at all levels of organization (ecosystems, species and genes). In applied conservation prioritization resources (monetary, personal, time) are often limited, and hence a smart and efficient way of taking quick and informative decisions is essential. One way to tackle these limitations is by using so called "biodiversity surrogates - measures of biodiversity that hopefully represent other levels of biodiversity ad well. So far, not much research is present about the use and efficacy of such biodiversity surrogates, lacking especially the genetic level of biodiversity.
